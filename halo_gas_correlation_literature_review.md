# Galaxy–CGM/IGM Clustering: One-Halo and Two-Halo Correlations in Absorption and Emission

*Literature review prepared for VDFI, July 2026*

## 1. Two families of method, and what "two-halo" actually means

Every paper below is trying to answer the same question — how is diffuse hydrogen distributed around galaxies, and out to what scale does a galaxy's influence (or its neighbors') extend? — but they get at it through two genuinely different observables, and it's worth being precise about which one you're fitting.

**Absorption / tomography.** You use background sightlines (single QSOs, QSO pairs, or dense grids of faint background galaxies as in CLAMATO/LATIS) and measure the Lyα forest transmission or optical depth as a function of transverse distance from a foreground galaxy. This is a *density/opacity* tracer — it is sensitive down to very low column densities and is the only method that cleanly reaches multi-Mpc scales, because you're not limited by surface-brightness dimming.

**Emission (stacking or direct imaging).** You measure the actual Lyα surface-brightness profile — from resonant scattering of galaxy-produced photons in the CGM, from recombination/collisional cooling radiation in filaments, or from fluorescence of the UV background off optically thick gas. This is what you're doing with your flux profile fit, and it directly gives you a photon budget, but it is surface-brightness-limited, so historically it only reached tens of kpc until MUSE/KCWI integral-field stacking pushed it to ~100 kpc and, in a few special cases, to Mpc-scale filaments.

**"One-halo" and "two-halo" terms.** This is standard halo-model language borrowed from large-scale-structure clustering (it shows up explicitly in the Lyα-forest × quasar/DLA BAO cross-correlation work, e.g. Cuceu et al. 2023, MNRAS 524, 1464) and it maps directly onto what you're seeing:
- The **one-halo term** is the correlation between a galaxy and gas that lives in its *own* dark-matter halo — CGM proper. It should track individual halo/circumgalactic physics (outflows, satellite accretion, virial radius) and dominates from galactocentric distances of a few kpc out to roughly the halo virial radius (~50–150 kpc at these redshifts and masses).
- The **two-halo term** is the correlation between a galaxy and gas associated with *neighboring* halos — i.e., large-scale structure/clustering bias. It dominates once you get beyond the virial radius, rises in amplitude with the galaxy's clustering bias, and asymptotically traces the linear (or mildly non-linear) matter correlation function — which is where "cosmic web" language becomes appropriate.

A profile (or correlation function) with two distinct scale lengths is exactly the signature you'd expect from a one-halo + two-halo decomposition, so the framing in your fit is well-motivated by the field's own vocabulary, not just an ad hoc double exponential.

## 2. Emission-side exponential fits (this is where "17 kpc" lives)

These papers fit **exponential surface-brightness profiles**, Σ(r) ∝ exp(−r/r_h), to stacked or individual Lyα halos — the direct analog of your first term.

- **Steidel et al. 2011** (ApJ 736, 160) — stacked narrowband Lyα imaging of 92 z~2–3 LBGs (Keck Baryonic Structure Survey/KBSS). Exponential scale lengths of **~25 kpc** for the full sample (25.2 kpc all galaxies, 25.6 kpc emission-line subset, 20.8 kpc absorption-line subset, 28.4 kpc for LAE-like sources), compared to a 3–4 kpc scale length for the UV continuum. This is the classic "diffuse halo is 5–10× larger than the stellar light" result, interpreted as resonant scattering of galaxy-produced Lyα in outflowing/infalling CGM gas.
- **Wisotzki et al. 2016** (A&A 587, A98) — first MUSE individual-galaxy stacking (HDFS, z=3–6): scale lengths **~1–7 kpc**, about 10× the UV half-light radius. Smaller than Steidel because these are individually much fainter, lower-mass galaxies than KBSS LBGs.
- **Leclercq et al. 2017** (A&A 608, A8, MUSE-HUDF Survey VIII) — 145 individual halos, 2-component circular-exponential decomposition: median scale length **~4.5 kpc**, a few objects >10 kpc.
- **Byrohl et al. 2021** (MNRAS 506, 5129) — full Lyα radiative-transfer post-processing of IllustrisTNG50, matched against the MUSE-UDF stacks above. Key physical result directly relevant to your two-component fit: they decompose the profile into (a) photons rescattered out of the galaxy's own star-forming region — dominates the inner profile — and (b) diffuse emission, and find that **the flattening of the profile at large radii is dominated by photons originating from *other, nearby halos*, not by diffuse emission from the galaxy's own gas.** That is a simulation-based, physically explicit statement of a two-halo term appearing in *emission* space, and it's the single closest analog I found to what you're likely seeing in your second component.

Your 17 kpc scale length sits comfortably inside the Steidel-to-Leclercq range (~5–25 kpc depending on sample selection/mass), and your note that your sample mixes LBGs and massive emission-line galaxies is exactly the kind of population mixing that shifts these numbers around in KBSS-like samples (Steidel's absorption vs. emission-line subsamples differ by ~8 kpc for that reason alone).

## 3. Absorption-side cross-correlations (tomography papers, and their scale-dependent structure)

These don't usually fit a single exponential to the whole profile; they measure the pixel optical depth or transmission as a function of (transverse, line-of-sight) separation, and the interesting physics shows up as *changes in behavior with scale*, which is conceptually the same information as your two exponential components.

- **Adelberger et al. 2003** (ApJ 584, 45) and **2005** (ApJ 629, 636) — first galaxy–IGM cross-correlation at z~3 (KBSS precursor). HI is *depleted* below the cosmic mean within ~0.5 h⁻¹ Mpc comoving of LBGs (attributed to outflows clearing the CGM) and *enhanced* just beyond that — already a two-scale signature, detected out to tens of comoving Mpc in the 2005 CIV follow-up.
- **Rakic et al. 2012** (ApJ 751, 94, KBSS) — first 2D optical-depth maps around 679 z~2.4 galaxies vs. 15 background QSOs. Median optical depth (density) drops by an order of magnitude around **~100 kpc** (close to the halo virial radius — your "one-halo" scale), but stays significantly enhanced out to **2.8 proper Mpc (~9 comoving Mpc)**. They also report two distinct redshift-space anisotropies: "finger-of-god" compression on <1 Mpc/<200 km/s scales (halo-internal kinematics — one-halo) and Kaiser-effect compression on 1.4–2.0 Mpc scales (large-scale coherent infall — two-halo). This is probably the cleanest observational precedent for "small-scale term + large-scale term with different physical origins" in this literature.
- **Turner et al. 2014** and the broader BOSS quasar–forest cross-correlation line (Font-Ribera 2013, 1303.1937) — same technique pushed to Mpc–100 Mpc scales around quasars; on >15 Mpc/h scales the cross-correlation matches the *linear* ΛCDM prediction, i.e. pure two-halo/large-scale-structure behavior with no need for any small-scale astrophysical term.
- **Sorini, Oñorbe, Hennawi & Lukić 2018** (ApJ 859, 125) — "A Fundamental Test for Galaxy Formation Models," the single most useful paper for your purposes: they explicitly stitch together small-scale (quasar pairs) and large-scale (BOSS) measurements to constrain the mean Lyα absorption profile continuously over **three decades in transverse distance, 20 kpc ≲ b ≲ 20 Mpc**, and compare to Illustris/Nyx simulations. Their finding: simulations *diverge* from each other and from observations on scales **20 kpc–2 Mpc** (galaxy-formation-physics-dependent — this is your one-halo-to-two-halo transition regime), but converge to a common, ΛCDM-determined profile beyond ~2 Mpc, where the "sphere of influence" of a galaxy (defined observationally, not via virial radius) turns out to extend to ~7× the halo virial radius, i.e. of order 700 kpc–1 Mpc. This gives you a literature-calibrated number for exactly where a one-halo → two-halo transition should sit.
- **Momose et al. 2021 / the CLAMATO galaxy-Lyα cross-correlation (GaLαCC) line**, most recently extended in **the 2025 CLAMATO×COSMOS re-analysis (arXiv:2509.07453, MNRAS 548, stag786)** — cross-correlates galaxies binned by stellar mass with the 3D CLAMATO Lyα forest tomographic map in COSMOS, and compares to cosmological hydrodynamic simulation predictions, finding real (few-Mpc-scale) discrepancies — i.e., the same one-halo/two-halo tension as Sorini et al., now in genuine 3D tomography rather than QSO-pencil-beam statistics.
- **Muzahid et al. 2021, Mukae et al. 2020, Chen et al. 2020** — related z~2–4 galaxy/LAE–HI cross-correlation studies (MUSE-based), consistently finding that galaxy *overdensity*/group membership boosts the absorption signal — i.e., the two-halo amplitude scales with environment, exactly as halo-model bias would predict.
- **Cuceu et al. 2023** (MNRAS 524, 1464) — technically a DLA/SBLA×Lyα-forest BAO paper, but it's a good example of the field's standard practice: the correlation function is modeled with an explicit physical template (linear/non-linear power spectrum + bias + RSD), not a bare exponential, once you're firmly in "two-halo"/large-scale-structure territory.

## 4. Direct detections of cosmic-web-scale emission (this is where "17 kpc + large scale" plausibly connects to literal filaments)

If your second exponential component turns out to be on the order of hundreds of kpc to several Mpc, this is the relevant comparison set — these are direct emission (not absorption) detections of structure well outside any single halo:

- **Umehata et al. 2019** (Science 366, 97) — Lyα filaments **>1 Mpc long** connecting galaxies/AGN in the SSA22 protocluster at z=3.1, imaged with Subaru/Suprime-Cam narrowband + follow-up. First direct imaging evidence for cosmic-web gas as opposed to a statistical inference.
- **Bacon et al. 2021** (A&A 647, A107, "MUSE Extremely Deep Field") — 140 hr MUSE stack over the HUDF: diffuse Lyα filaments on **2.5–4 comoving Mpc** scales at z=3.1–4.5, surface brightness ~5×10⁻²⁰ erg/s/cm²/arcsec². Critically, **70% of the filament Lyα luminosity comes from outside the CGM of any identified LAE** — i.e., genuinely diffuse/two-halo-scale emission, not one-halo emission from many faint undetected galaxies. They also show fluorescence from the UV background alone can supply at most ~10–34% of the observed luminosity, so a large population of ultra-faint (SFR <10⁻⁴ M⊙/yr) LAEs, clustered together, is invoked to make up the rest.
- **A more recent MUSE Ultra Deep Field paper (2024/2025, arXiv:2412.06895)** extends this to a continuous **5 Mpc stretch** of z≈4 cosmic web in emission — worth checking directly since it's very recent (within your reach given your July 2026 "now").
- **Witstok et al. / "Prospects for observing the low-density cosmic web in Lyα emission" (2021, A&A)** — theoretical treatment of what surface-brightness sensitivity is needed to detect the *low-density*, non-filament cosmic web directly, as opposed to the denser filaments Umehata/Bacon already caught.

## 5. What number people actually quote to "quantify correlation"

This is worth separating out because the answer changes depending on which observable you're asking about, and it bears directly on your "is there a single general number" question:

- For **surface-brightness/flux radial profiles** (your case, Steidel/Wisotzki/Leclercq/Byrohl above): the standard number *is* an **exponential scale length**, usually in kpc, sometimes reported instead as a half-light radius. This is the correct genre for what you fit.
- For the **galaxy–galaxy (or galaxy–gas) two-point correlation function** in the large-scale-structure sense, the standard practice is actually a **power law**, ξ(r) = (r/r₀)^(−γ), not an exponential — reported as a correlation length r₀ (in Mpc/h) and slope γ. Representative numbers: r₀ ≈ 5.05 Mpc/h, γ ≈ 1.67 for 2dFGRS galaxies at low z; r₀ ≈ 1.1–1.6 Mpc/h with steeper γ ≈ 2.0–2.5 for z~3 LBGs (this steepening/high-r₀ behavior at high z is itself interpreted as a bias effect — z~3 star-forming galaxies sit in comparatively massive, less common halos). The two-halo term of a full halo-model fit is what actually explains *why* the power law holds and lets you extract galaxy bias b(z) directly.
- Bias itself, b = √(ξ_galaxy/ξ_matter) or equivalently from the amplitude of the two-halo term relative to the (known) linear matter correlation function, is the number that most directly connects an observed correlation amplitude to "how strongly does this population trace the cosmic web," and it's the number several of the cross-correlation papers above (Momose, Chen, Muzahid) use to compare galaxy subpopulations.

So: exponential fits are the right and standard tool for your flux/surface-brightness profile; if you ever want to connect your result to the *cosmic web* literature in the strict clustering sense, the natural translation is not "convert kpc to Mpc" but rather "the amplitude and scale of my second exponential component is playing the role that r₀, γ, and b play in the standard two-point correlation function — it's the observable manifestation of the same two-halo physics, expressed as a radial emission profile instead of a correlation function."

## 6. How this maps onto your result, and how to pin down "what's causing it"

Given what's above, three physically distinct (and non-exclusive) origins could produce a second, larger exponential component in a stacked Lyα flux/surface-brightness profile, and the literature gives you concrete ways to tell them apart:

1. **Genuine one-halo → two-halo transition (galaxy clustering / neighboring halos).** Byrohl et al. 2021 explicitly predict this: outer-profile flattening dominated by photons from *other* halos in TNG50. Sorini et al. 2018 independently calibrate the transition scale at order ~0.7–2 Mpc (the "~7× virial radius" number). *Test:* stack separately in bins of local galaxy overdensity/environment (as Muzahid/Mukae/Chen do for absorption) — if the second component's amplitude scales with environment density, it's two-halo/clustering, not a property of the individual galaxy.
2. **Diffuse cosmic-web emission not tied to individual halos at all** (fluorescence off the UV background, or truly diffuse recombination emission from filament gas). Bacon et al. 2021 found this dominates *outside* any known LAE's CGM but *cannot* be fully explained by UV-background fluorescence alone (≤34%) — they need a large ultra-faint LAE population instead. *Test:* compare your second-component surface brightness/amplitude to the fluorescence-only prediction (a computable number from the UV background intensity at your redshift) — if you exceed it, you need either clustered faint sources or non-fluorescent power (cooling radiation, star formation in low-mass satellites).
3. **Instrumental/systematic large-scale background** (scattered light, sky subtraction residuals, or continuum-fitting bias in the case of absorption tomography) — always worth ruling out first, especially since the "three decades in distance" papers (Sorini et al.) exist precisely because getting the large-scale asymptote right is notoriously easy to get subtly wrong.

Practically, the field's playbook for distinguishing these (Rakic 2012's redshift-space anisotropy split; Byrohl's radiative-transfer decomposition; Bacon's environment/overdensity binning) all boil down to the same idea: don't just fit the radial profile in isolation — cross it with a second axis (velocity/redshift-space distortion, local environment density, or a physically-motivated null model like UV-background fluorescence) to see which physical mechanism the amplitude actually tracks.

Your 17 kpc component landing right in the Steidel/KBSS range, from a KBSS-like mixed LBG+MELG sample, is a good consistency check on the one-halo side. Whatever scale the second component sits at will tell you a lot on its own: order ~100 kpc–1 Mpc points to the one-halo/two-halo transition (halo environment, per Sorini/Byrohl); several Mpc points toward the Umehata/Bacon-style direct cosmic-web-filament regime, in which case the relevant comparison sample is protocluster/overdense-field imaging, not stacked field-galaxy CGM studies.

**Term 2 (h2 = 1552 ± 548 kpc).** Well beyond the one-halo/CGM regime — R_vir marked ≈75 kpc, so h2 is ~20× that — and, converted to comoving units at a typical KBSS redshift, lands close to the only two direct-imaging detections of the cosmic web in Lyα emission currently in the literature: Bacon et al. 2021 (2.5–4 comoving Mpc) and the 2024/2025 MUSE Ultra Deep Field 5 Mpc filament result (§4). But the *ordering* — projected slope (0.8) fits best, raw 3D slope (1.8) fits worst — matches what you'd predict from the line-of-sight window: your ±3Å (≈740–800 km/s half-width) spectral window corresponds to a ~20 Mpc comoving (~14 Mpc/h) line-of-sight depth at z=2–3, several times any typical LAE/LBG correlation length (r0 ≈ 2.5–4.5 h⁻¹Mpc; Bielby et al. 2016, MNRAS 456, 4061), i.e. deep enough that the Limber approximation (and its γ→γ−1 slope shift) should actually apply, rather than the raw 3D slope.

**Two different physically meaningful radii come out of the expcore fit, not one:**
- **Crossover radius** (`crossover_radius_expcore` — where term1=term2, the actual "one-halo term stops dominating" radius): **≈76 kpc**, essentially identical to R_vir and to the two-exponential model's own crossover (`crossover_radius_twoexp` ≈ 76.0 kpc). This is the number that maps onto the one-halo/two-halo language of §1, and it's robust across both functional forms.
- **r_c** (the expcore model's own internal softening scale — where the outer term itself turns from flat to power-law-declining, NOT the same radius as the crossover above): **488 ± 211 kpc**, i.e. ~6.5× R_vir. This has no reason to match R_vir — it's a different quantity — but it lines up well with **Sorini, Oñorbe, Hennawi & Lukić 2018** (ApJ 859, 125), who calibrate a galaxy's "sphere of influence" (where the mean Lyα absorption profile converges onto the pure ΛCDM/linear-theory prediction) at ~7× R_vir (~700 kpc–1 Mpc for their sample). For R_vir=75 kpc, 7×R_vir = 525 kpc — within 1σ of the fitted r_c, and via a completely different observable (Sorini: absorption/tomography; this pipeline: emission stacking). Independent-method agreement like that is a stronger check than anything internal to one fit.
- Also relevant: **Byrohl et al. 2021** (MNRAS 506, 5129, TNG50 radiative transfer) find the outer stacked-Lyα-profile flattening is dominated by photons scattered from *other* halos (companions/satellites), not diffuse gas from the galaxy's own CGM — onset around ≥30 pkpc in their simulation, order-of-magnitude consistent with (if somewhat smaller than) this fit's ~76 kpc crossover, plausibly reflecting TNG50's lower-mass galaxy population vs. this KBSS-like sample.
