- HSC g band seems to work the best for magnitude calculation
- We attempt to match a given source to a source-extracted position in an HSC image, and then measure the magnitude at varying radii
- We chose the 2" radius for all sources
- To separate OII and LAEs, we use a decision plane in 3D. The plane is created by matching with HETDEX detections that have either LAE or OII classifications, calculating the HSC g-band flux, wave of emission line, and lineflux. We plot this in 3D and train a separator to best distinguish the two known clouds of data.
- The plane is able to distinguish with a 98% success rate, with some of the 2% being detections that HETDEX misclassified
- We ran ALL VDFI sources and determined if they are more like OIIs or LAEs.

- We also plotted the spatial offset each source has when matching with a source found in the HSC images
- For matched with HETDEX, most sources group towards being offset by +/- 1 arcsecond
- In the total VDFI sample, however, the offset is more of a double gaussian, with many being similar to HETDEX matches, and many having much further match separation.
- There are about 32.2% of sources that are part of the closer matched gaussian, and 67.8% are part of the wide, random match gaussian. This gives us a number hinting to the total number of real/false detections in our data.

Investigating LAE and AGN Properties:
-------------------------------------
- Statistically significant LAE properties, especially at the faint end — measuring how the faint Lyα population behaves and how it connects to continuum magnitude, stellar mass, and other multiwavelength counterparts in COSMOS. This includes understanding how Lyα strength scales with continuum and whether faint LAEs represent a distinct physical population or simply the low-luminosity extension of brighter systems.
- AGN properties, with an emphasis on low-continuum and faint AGN — identifying emission-line–dominated or weak-continuum AGN that may be missed in traditional selections, and characterizing their luminosities, environments, and overlap with LAE-selected galaxies.
- AGN–LAE cross-correlation — measuring how LAEs cluster around AGN (and vice versa) to understand environmental effects, relative halo masses, and whether AGN activity influences Lyα visibility.
- AGN turnoff and mixed LAE/AGN systems — investigating galaxies that show properties of both populations, looking for transitional or composite objects, and exploring how black hole activity and Lyα emission coexist or evolve within the same systems.


Given your dataset — deep spectroscopy, nearly full fill factor, COSMOS field, optimized for LAEs and [O II] — you’re in a very strong position. The key is to leverage **(1) completeness, (2) depth, and (3) multiwavelength overlap**. Here’s how each science goal is best executed with what you have.

---
Faint LAE Properties — Do This First (Low Hanging Fruit + High Impact)

Your biggest advantage over HETDEX-like surveys is **depth + contiguous coverage**. That means:

Best Strategy
* Construct a **carefully completeness-corrected LAE catalog**.
  * Injection–recovery simulations for line flux.
  * Spatial completeness maps (since you’re nearly full fill factor, this is powerful).

* Measure the **Lyα luminosity function in multiple redshift bins**.
  * Push the faint end.
  * Compare directly to HETDEX, MUSE, narrowband surveys.

* Cross-match to COSMOS photometry.
  * UV magnitude vs Lyα luminosity.
  * EW distributions vs stellar mass.
  * Look for trends in dust proxies.

Why this works well for you:

* You reduce sampling variance.
* You probe below wide-field survey limits.
* COSMOS gives stellar mass, SFR, morphology essentially “for free.”

This should likely be your **first major paper**.
---
Identifying AGN — Use Multiwavelength Leverage

Because you’re in COSMOS, you should not rely on spectra alone.

Step-by-step:

* Identify AGN spectroscopically:
  * Broad lines (if any)
  * High-ionization UV lines
  * Line width measurements
* Cross-match with:
  * X-ray catalogs
  * Radio
  * IR AGN selections
* Flag:
  * Clear AGN
  * Likely AGN
  * Possible composite systems

Then examine:
* Fraction of LAEs hosting AGN.
* How AGN fraction changes with Lyα luminosity.
* Whether faint AGN are preferentially Lyα-selected.

Your dataset is especially good for **emission-line–dominated AGN**, which broadband surveys can miss.
---
AGN–LAE Cross-Correlation — Requires Careful Statistics

Because your area is roughly moon-sized (~0.2–0.3 deg²), this is feasible but must be done carefully.

Best approach:
* Compute 2D projected correlation functions.
* Use Landy–Szalay estimator.
* Generate realistic random catalogs including:
  * Spatial completeness variations.
  * Redshift selection function.

What you can measure:
* Relative bias between AGN and LAEs.
* Whether LAEs are enhanced around AGN.
* Environmental modulation of Lyα emission.

This becomes stronger once your LAE catalog is robust.
---
Transitional LAE–AGN Systems — Needs Spectral Diagnostics

This is more nuanced and comes after classification work.

What to look for:
* Moderate line widths.
* High-ionization lines.
* Elevated Lyα luminosity for given continuum.
* X-ray weak but line-strong systems.

You can:
* Stack spectra of ambiguous systems.
* Compare line ratios statistically.
* Look at environmental dependence of composite systems.

This is likely a **second- or third-paper topic**, once catalogs are stable.
---
What Is Most Powerful With Your Dataset?

Ranked by feasibility + impact:
1. **Faint-end LAE luminosity function (high confidence, high impact)**
2. **Lyα–continuum–stellar mass scaling relations**
3. **AGN fraction within LAEs**
4. **AGN–LAE cross-correlation**
5. **Transitional systems / AGN turnoff**
---
Critical Technical Things You Must Do Well
* Precise line-flux calibration.
* Accurate redshift classification (Lyα vs [O II] contamination).
* Selection function modeling.
* Cosmic variance estimation (your field size matters here).
